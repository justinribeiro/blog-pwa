Some years ago, I acquired—in perfect working order—a [dumpster](/chronicle/2019/08/02/that-time-i-found-a-leica-iiic-in-a-dumpster/) Leica IIIc and Summitar lens. It was dumb luck then as it is now and I love a user-camera.

Little did I know that I would find myself enamored by the Summitar. My version is a 10-blade, single coated version post World War II, in the 621000 serial number range. Frankly none of that metadata matters; with old gear as in new, I just load up some film or stick it on a body, make some photographs, and then determine if it brought the vision I had to life. If you really want the specs, [Ken Rockwell](https://kenrockwell.com/leica/50mm-f2-summitar.htm) of course has done a much better job of that.

Regardless, I have used this lens and body without issue for a long time at this point. It's always one of the camera combinations I keep in ready-to-shot mode within arms length. That said, the last few rolls I've struggled with the friction on the aperture ring. This is not uncommon; old grease needs replacing and the warmer spring weather had not freed the ring as in years past.

Luckily, this is a simple operation. While not all versions of this lens are the same, the general operation is captured very well in this [video from Libsenth](https://youtu.be/yxBr6lkS2oc) does a nice job. For my version, simply removing the front element and the aperture ring via the two very tiny machine screws was all that was required.

I used nothing more than a cotton cleaning tip and a very tiny amount of glass cleaner; I stay away from the harsh cleaning solvents and brass. As you can see in the photo above, the cotton tip does not lie—that ring needed a cleaning. With a bit of added silicon lubricate so we don't end up with travel into other places in the lens, I reattached the ring, aligned, and tested. Smooth as silk.

I love a quick fix. Now, back to making some photographs with this lens.
