It started at an estate sale. Monica and I were driving by and we decided to stop in and take a peek—a gander, if you will. We're never specifically looking to walk away with anything, but I am endlessly looking for old parts for the various cameras, darkroom, and shop machines to repair or to just keep things running. This sale had nothing of particular interest, but had a lot of battered and rusted tools in the garage. Among them, two No. 4 smoothing hand planes I found in a pile of other tools.

I'm not building nor doing a lot of fine woodworking projects but I adore working, usable tools. As hand planes go I have a few I've found over the years and while I may only use them sparingly, when you need one, you need one. These planes were however in rough shape and not usable.

I had not restored or repaired a hand plane before. Did I desperately need to undertake this? No. Where these hand planes of some magical collectable value making it economically worth the endeavor? Definitely not; I may not know much about planes, but one of those above is a Shelton and even I know they barely worked when they were new.

But it might fun. So let's restore the unknown No. 4 because joy.

## A Mystery No. 4

Fun fact, a lot of companies made Bailey-style planes. If you have a cursory or passing knowledge of woodworking, you'd recognize a Stanley No. 4 even if you didn't quite know what it was. There entire books on the subject, but if you're really interested in nitty gritter planes, see [The Superior Works: Patrick's Blood and Gore](https://www.supertool.com/StanleyBG/stan0a.html)—yes, that is the real name.

The more interesting of the two planes seemingly had no makers mark, nor did it have a lever cap. Eyeballing the images, I did find an [old thread from 2007](https://www.finewoodworking.com/forum/identifying-an-old-hand-plane) on FineWoodworking that had a similar-ish pictures that it may be a Sargent. Sargent made decent tools and while I couldn't find any more specific information on it, it was enough to go; Bailey-style planes have generally the same sort of components.

## A Non-exhaustive Journey

If you're looking for a step-by-step guide, this is not it. There are lots of better places that will go into finer details—see [Workshop Companion](https://workshopcompanion.com/) or his [video on fine tuning a plane](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Szan5pP4drQ)—this is simply my stumbling approach to getting a rusted plane back into a working tool.

Given the level of rust and grime, I started with my tried-and-true method of using letting the various components sit in [Evapo-Rust](https://www.crcindustries.com/evapo-rust/) for a day, scrub clean and evaluate, and then repeat the process until a good start point could be had. In the case of most of the components this took a couple days.

While that was happening, I needed to track down a lever cap and lever cap screw. The plane was missing these and in their place sat a screw and washer that appeared to be someone's attempt an emergency fix. How this plane cut with this in place I'm not sure, but after digging through various listings on Ebay, I eventually found a lever cap and screw that appeared to be for either a Sargent or a Lakeside No. 4.

Next was the fact that someone had at one point painted the frog adjuster. One should not do this; it creates an uneven seat for your blade and leads to other problems (e.g., chattering). A straight edge confirmed things needed filing, so careful work with a set of files brought the face back to true.

Finally, one of the nicer parts of this plane was that it had a lot of cutting steel left. If it had been maintained or sharpened over time I'd be surprised; it simply did not appear to have been the case. I use a similar approach for [sharpening planes and chisels as Paul Sellers](https://paulsellers.com/2024/10/low-cost-long-lasting-sharpening-kit/), using three grits plates (350, 800, 1200) and a leather strop. Getting an edge back onto the steel took about 15 minutes.

With the plane metal parts in working shape, I finished the plane handles by sanding the flaking and failing red paint and applying some fresh oil and lacquer to really get a nice shine.

## A Workable Tool

All said and done, I spent longer waiting for the replacement parts than it took to actually bring this plane back into working order. Parts wise, I also only spent $20-ish USD to restore the plane to working condition; a huge thank you to Ebay sellers out there selling the small, random parts that make these rebuilds possible.

Yes, I could have polished this plane a more or did a little more to really tune it in. But for me, it was a good learning experience on a topic I knew little about and in the end I have a nice workable plane that I can use for the odd job here and there.

And you know I love old tools. 😊
