Given the my recent completion of our [workbench build](/chronicle/2026/02/19/building-a-new-workbench-art-table-for-the-shop/) and my [love of all things vises](/chronicle/2026/01/28/making-a-custom-base-for-the-panavise-model-201-vise/), it will shock no one that I decided to add a bench vise to the new workbench. I looked around at off-the-shelf new options but lets be honest, that's not my style.

Instead, having seen a no-name rusted and seized small woodworking vice at an estate sales some months back—I regret having not attempted to recover that one off that bench—I decided to try my hand at locating something on the small side to add to the bench. I love big, heavy vises, but I really wanted something small and useable, something that was largely out of the way and that I possibly would add more of—one on each side.

Whilst digging through Ebay, I ran across a Sears Craftsman vise I had not seen before, Model 391.5190. From what I could piece together from a thin set of information, it was originally manufactured in Japan and measuring 6 1/2 inches wide by 2 1/4 deep with 4 1/2 inch wide opening was marketed as a lightweight vise for beginner woodworkers. I couldn't find the original manual—the vises' bigger brother, Model 391.5195, is available in the [Internet Archive](https://archive.org/details/sears-craftsman-woodworkers-vise-391.5195)—but it looked about the size I was looking for. Better yet, there were a fair amount of them listed, were affordable at around $30-40 USD in not terribly beat up condition.

Having picked one up from Ebay, I found it to have little more than surface rust. Since I had a bucket already filled, I took the vice apart and let sit in [Evapo-Rust](https://www.crcindustries.com/evapo-rust/) for a day, and then pulled the parts and washed them clean. There was no real damage needing of repair; I sanded and filed any sharp spots and prepared for a coat of enamel.

At this point it was time to install it on the bench. While I didn't have the manual, it was fairly clear given the mounting holes how this would work. Given that my bench top was only one inch and a quarter thick, I was going to need an additional plate attached to the underside so that the vise jaws would not sit proud of the bench top. The 391.5195 manual noted that the plate should be roughly four inches by ten inches for that larger vice, but this seemed appreciate even in this case. I grabbed a piece of spare poplar, cut it size, marked and pre-drilled the piece and the bench and the attached the bench face of the vise.

While I won't be mistaken as a woodworker any time soon, I do love Paul Sellers and a lot of his guides. Subsequently, instead of mounting the jaws flush to the bench the underside plate actually leaves the metal jaws of the vice around a 1/8 of inch below the bench surface. From there, I followed a similar path that Paul outlines in his [On Vises—Part III](https://paulsellers.com/2016/06/vises-part-iii/) post. I cut and wrapped the bench-side jaws with poplar—I had it on hand and it works great in this application—oversizing an inch on each side then hand planned the poplar flat to the bench.

As a last step, given some of the things we work on, I knew that I wanted at least one soft face. I chose leather if only again I had spare leather from a different project and wrapped the winding jaws face using double sided tape. I should have used contact cement—the bond would have been better—so I ended up tacking the leather to outside face using spare stainless steel nails that I cut to length.

From a use standpoint I love the small size of this vise—so much so I just bought another one off of Ebay to restore and mount to the other side. While it may not be the vise I turned to when banging on metal or planning a large piece of timber, I have other vises for those tasks. This size I find it to be perfect for the sort of small things we work on, everything from holding a figure for a quick bit of paint, to holding our matboard cutter which used to endlessly slide with the tiniest touch of force. No more with this little edition to the workshop.

The next time you see an old vise, look twice. You may just have a spot for it on your bench.
