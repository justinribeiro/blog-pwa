This is not a review so much as my shocked amazement of [Bambu Lab P2S](https://bambulab.com/en-us/p2s). I've used a lot of 3D printers over the years—like, 15 years as I recall—including writing one of the first internet-connected views of status into ReplicatorG via MQTT which I spoke about at various conferences over the years. Yes, I am that old.

The struggle over the years was that while I've seen leaps and bounds in software and slicers, the printers have left me disappointed. Not that they didn't work or weren't workable—they were quite good—but they weren't accessible to the average person. I watched with saddened eyes of the claims of turnkey solutions that were anything but, frustrated buyers expressing their frustration online, with many of printers sitting idle and unused.

Fast forward to a cold wet day of random viewing of YouTube maker projects—the twins and I have been watching a lot of [Emily The Engineer](https://www.youtube.com/c/EmilyTheEngineer) lately—I happened to notice her lab of printers in one of her more recent videos were primarily Bambu Lab printers. A few other videos from other makers and I begin to notice a trend of Bambu Lab sponsoring a lot of those videos, even if not 3D printing specific.

Color me intrigued.

After doing some light reading, eyeballing the specs, and poking at the software, I decided to again roll the dice in the 3D printer space and meet the growing calls in my household for one of these majestic tools. Which is the short story of how we ended up with a [Bambu Lab P2S](https://bambulab.com/en-us/p2s) and their AMS 2 Pro—automatic material system as it were—showing up at the house.

The setup of the machine claims you can have it running in about 15 minutes. We found this to be true; it was quick and easy to setup on a spare machine table I had and before you know it the twins were printing a [3Dbenchy](https://www.3dbenchy.com/). Having printed hundreds of benchy boats over the years, this machine—out of the box, no tuning required mind you—printed a clean version that left me speechless. Was it perfect to the purveyors of gates to access this 3D printing technology? No, I've seen the complaints. Did my kids or I care? Of course not; it was refreshing to not have to tune this machine.

With the test complete, the twins started printing. And printing. And printing more. That was last Friday—the printer has printed nearly continuously for a week. Have I had to clean the nozzle or handle broken filament? Nope. While the rare retraction error on the filament spools pops up on some of their multi-color prints, unlike so many other printers that would have ruined hours of a print, the machine alerts you, you fix the issue, and it just continues.

Better still: the twins can do that. It does not require intervention from myself or Monica.

This is in large part why I call it magical; all the ease of use issues that plagued so many printers I've tried over the years seemingly vanish with not only this printer but I'm told many of the newer style of printers. The applications for mobile and desktop are easy to use; the twins rarely do anything put send the next print from their phone. No dance of math and mechanics to make a print successful. No pre-treating the surface with tape or glue.

While the kids are having fun printing, I'm reveling in the ease of use and the clean prints this machine makes. This is what I had hoped for when I started printing so long ago, something that wasn't for the technically minded but off-the-shelf usable. This is the closest I've seen to date, and I am thrilled.

Again, this isn't a review—there are lots on the internet for your nitty-gritty details of this machine. Frankly, while I hope these words convey my overarching joy of watching my kids have fun and starting to explore another creative space, I fear I may fall short. Regardless, if like me your 3D printing experience has biased you against revisiting the field, I would say it's time to look again.
