At the end of last year with the [darkroom largely complete](/chronicle/2025/08/08/the-build-journey-to-a-new-darkroom/), I turned my attention to start looking at the larger shop/art room and the setup. The room itself was still a hodge-podge of my tool boxes and tools, with various art carts in various parts of the room. While this worked for me, it was not working for anyone else in the house. Need to work on a project? Haul out one of the paint-covered spare folding tables.

No animosity for the folding tables—they're very useful—just not great when you need something to not move around on you or you need more space.

I therefore set out to build a work table—or bench, or art table if you prefer—that would work for the majority of the household.

## Need Ideas? Ask People About Their Workbench

Workbenches and art tables come in a lot of shapes and sizes. I know this because I became overwhelmed with the shear volume of opinions on the matter. Commercial versions, free plan versions, specific workflow types—you name it, there is someone willing to discuss their table with you. What I found is not surprising; people build or modify tables that work for them and their gear.

What did our table need to be? The kids needed space to work on their projects—they spread out—and had to handle our matte cutter which I use for mounting and framing prints; it was going to need to be on the larger side. The table should have space for the immediate needs parts-wise; can I get to that oddball set of screws in one of our part boxes without having to run to the parts shed? It needed to be weighty yet moveable around the space, sort of like a woodworking bench or a welding table.

And as inexpensive as I can make it. Simple requirements.

## Using What We Have

There were two other requirements, which is really just a requirement on every project for me, which is what-do-we-have-already. A large part of the design was dictated by our collection of older no-longer-made [Stanley parts boxes](https://www.stanleytools.com/products/storage/tool-organizers-bins) (~24) and [Akro-Mils AkroBins](https://akro-mils.com/products/storage-bins/) (~24) and panels. There was no reason to replace these—regardless of my lust for the Sortimo T-BOXXs—as they work without fail and aren't on the verge of failure.

## Getting What We Do Not

What I did not have was lumber. Like, none. This is an odd case for me; I recover and keep lumber of various types but given the darkroom build and several other builds yet to be written up, I was down to a single scrap 2x4. So purchasing actual wood would be required.

Do enough reading and you'll find heated debates and arguments around the materials of any workbench. I've worked on a lot of different tables and benches over the years and they all have their pros/cons. In terms of this build, I was leaning towards cost effective approaches similar to [The Anarchist Workbench](https://blog.lostartpress.com/2020/06/22/the-anarchists-workbench-for-free-forever/) which uses wood like Douglas Fir. On top of that, I didn't want an open bench, I needed storage for those parts and a tool shelf for working, so a plywood center would be useful.

Sounded workable and inexpensive. Let's build it.

## Building the Legs

I decided to go for broke on the weight out of the gate by using 4x6 inch Douglas Fir. I did go through the stack to find more dry pieces as opposed to the soaked wood that one usually gets. In terms of time saving, after wandering the aisle and letting my creativity bubble for a while, I decided that instead of joinery I wood use glue, clamps, and [FastenMaster TimberLOK](https://www.fastenmaster.com/products/timberlok) structural screws. I had used these screws in the past for building actual structures, and this would save time but also be incredibly strong. I did mark and pre-drill the entry points given the length (10 inches long).

## Building the Inner Shelving

The inner shelving would be a little more tricky then the legs. I didn't want to use more than two sheets of plywood as I had opted for nicer 3/4 inch birch, as I was not going to veneer the plywood layers—I like that layer look. This combined with the double sided approach I was taking—parts boxes on one side, bins on the other—meant I also very much needed to not make a classic Justin-forgot-the-board-edge-width when laying this out.

The general layout would account for roughly 48 inches long with 28 inches wide while being about 2 inches shorter than the legs giving clearance on the ground like a cabinet kick. Handling the draws first, I laid out a grid to allow the cases to slide in and out on drawer runners. The old Stanley boxes have a strong but very thin edge lip on the case, so I used 1/4 inch pine strips as the runner which allowed me the clearance required.

The cases have about an 1/8 inch clearance on each side of the case and the slide action is quite lovely. The trick of course is you have to square it; go to fast and you'll have a bad experience as with any drawer build.

The bins by all accounts were much easier that the parts boxes. Akro-Mils makes a mounting panel, which I had from the previous install spot in the parts shed, so simply mounting that made the bin install quick and painless.

## Finishing the Table Top

With the legs and inner shelves finished, I debated what to use as the table top for longer than I care to admit. To build it via cut down 2x12s would be fine, but it was also the holidays and I had other things to do. I browsed [McMaster-Carr](https://www.mcmaster.com/products/tabletops/), which has some lovely table tops. For the size I was looking for though 72 inches by 30 inches the prices were a bit too much, so I looked for spare butcher-block style tops. What I settled on was an inch and a quarter thick butcher-block of acacia wood, which I scored for a little shy of $300 USD. Acacia is a very hard wood and should take the wear and tear well enough.

With the top installed it also needed to be sealed. I did this by mixing a 1:1:1 ratio of boiled linseed oil, mineral spirits, and an oil-based poly. I like this mixture as it's easy to use and requires only light sanding between coats—though I would be remiss to not mention there is a great blog post on this regarding [various woodworking finish mixtures](https://www.bwwt.us/finishes-for-woodturning.html) complied from a lot of sources.

I should also note I added flip up wheels to be able to move it around. This was because that requirement for weight and a sturdy frame was met and then some; this thing is impossible to move without wheels. I'm not a huge fan of the wheels but they work well enough to get the table around the shop, particularly to use it as an outfeed.

## Not Quite Finished

In the current form it's in, it's a very workable table. The table has seen a lot of use since it's inception and has the paint and stains to prove it. I love that about tables in a workshop; they show not the scars but the beauty of creative processes.

There are a few additional things I'll be adding to it, particularly a small vice or two for small jobs and a bit of power to make some things easier. But for all said and done, just shy of $650 USD on the build, we've got a nice working table. Or workbench. Or structure. Look, it doesn't fall over when you use it, that's the important part. Workshop goals!
